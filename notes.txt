

sample space

In Probability Theory, the sample space is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. An experiment is any process that gives a result, like tossing a coin or rolling a die. Each outcome in the sample space is assigned a probability. A group of one or more outcomes from the sample space is called an event.Types of Sample SpacesFinite: The number of outcomes is countable and limited (e.g., rolling a die).$S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$Infinite: The outcomes go on forever (e.g., the number of coin tosses needed until the first head appears).$S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, \dots\}$Continuous: The outcomes can take any real value within a given range (e.g., a real number between 0 and 1).$S = \{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid 0 \le x \le 1\}$Specific Experiment Sample SpacesTossing CoinsWhen Two Coins are Tossed: Each coin results in Head (H) or Tail (T). There are 4 outcomes:$\{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$When Three Coins are Tossed: Calculated as $2^3 = 8$ outcomes:$\{HHH, HHT, HTH, THH, HTT, THT, TTH, TTT\}$Rolling DiceWhen One Die is Rolled: 6 outcomes.$S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$When Two Dice are Rolled: Consists of pairs ranging from $(1, 1)$ to $(6, 6)$, totaling $6^2 = 36$ pairs.Solved ExamplesExample 1: How many possible outcomes when rolling a fair six-sided die?Solution: 6.Example 2: In a deck of 52 playing cards, how many ways to draw two cards without replacement?Solution: $\binom{52}{2} = 1,326$.Example 3: If you flip a coin three times, how many elements are in the sample space?Solution: $2^3 = 8$.Example 4: A jar contains 20 red and 30 blue marbles. If you draw two marbles without replacement, how many different pairs (RR, BB, RB) can you get?Solution: Total marbles = 50. Total ways to choose any pair: $\binom{50}{2} = \frac{50 \times 49}{2} = 1225$.Example 5: For a 4-digit PIN code (digits 0-9), how many combinations?Solution: $10^4 = 10,000$.Practice Problems & AnswersProblem: Flip a coin two times, how many elements in $S$?Answer: 4Problem: Outcomes for rolling two fair six-sided dice?Answer: 36Problem: Ways to draw four cards from 52 without replacement?Answer: 270,725Problem: Ways to draw two cards from 52 with replacement?Answer: 2,704 ($52 \times 52$)Problem: Possible combinations for a 3-digit PIN (0-9)?Answer: 1,000 Note: Always check if the experiment is "with replacement" or "without replacement." This changes your sample space size ($|S|$) from a simple power ($n^k$) to a combination ($\binom{n}{k}$), which is a common pitfall in ML combinatorics.

types-of-events-in-probability

An event is a particular outcome or a collection of outcomes from the sample space ($S$). Events are subsets of the sample space.Types of EventsImpossible EventDefinition: Events that cannot happen; described by an empty set $\emptyset$.Example: Rolling a die and getting a multiple of 7. $S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$, so $E = \emptyset$.Sure Event (Certain Event)Definition: An event that consists of the entire sample space; probability is 1.Example: Rolling a die and getting a number less than 7.Simple EventDefinition: Comprises a single result from the sample space.Example: Rolling a die and getting a number less than 2 ($E = \{1\}$).Compound EventDefinition: Comprises more than a single result from the sample space.Example: Rolling a die and getting $\{3, 4, 5\}$.Dependent EventsDefinition: The next outcome depends on previous outcomes; probability changes.Example: Drawing a black ball from a bag (4 black, 3 red) without replacement.1st draw: $P(\text{black}) = 4/7$.2nd draw (if 1st was black): $P(\text{black}) = 3/6$.Independent EventsDefinition: The next outcome is independent of the previous one; probability remains the same.Example: Rolling a die multiple times. $P(\text{even number})$ is always $0.5$.Equally Likely EventsDefinition: Outcomes that have the same probability of occurring.Example: Rolling a fair six-sided die; each number has a $1/6$ chance.Mutually Exclusive EventsDefinition: Events that cannot happen at the same time ($A \cap B = \emptyset$).Example: Rolling a die and getting both a 2 and a 5 on the same roll.Exhaustive EventsDefinition: A set of events that cover all possible outcomes of an experiment.Example: Tossing a coin; Event A (Heads) and Event B (Tails) together cover the whole sample space.Solved ProblemsProblem 1 (Two coins): $A = \text{at least one head}$, $B = \text{no head}$. Are they mutually exclusive?Solution: $S = \{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$. $A = \{HH, HT, TH\}$, $B = \{TT\}$. $A \cap B = \emptyset$. Yes, they are mutually exclusive.Problem 2 (Die rolled twice): $A = \text{sum is 7}$, $B = \text{first die is 4}$. Find $A \cap B$.Solution: $A = \{(1,6), (2,5), (3,4), (4,3), (5,2), (6,1)\}$. $B = \{(4,1), (4,2), (4,3), (4,4), (4,5), (4,6)\}$. $A \cap B = \{(4,3)\}$.Problem 3 (Card deck): $A = \text{drawing a king}$, $B = \text{drawing a red card}$. Find $A \cap B$ and $A \cup B$.Solution: $A \cap B = \{K\heartsuit, K\diamondsuit\}$ (2 cards). $A \cup B = 26 \text{ red cards} + 2 \text{ black kings} = 28 \text{ cards}$.Problem 4 (Two dice): $A = \text{sum is even}$, $B = \text{both numbers even}$. Are they mutually exclusive?Solution: If both numbers are even, the sum is always even. Thus $A \cap B$ is not empty. No, they are not mutually exclusive. Note: Don't confuse Mutually Exclusive with Independent. Mutually exclusive means they can't happen together ($P(A \cap B) = 0$), while Independent means one happening doesn't change the odds of the other ($P(A|B) = P(A)$). In fact, mutually exclusive events are almost always dependent!


probability-rules

1. Addition RuleDeals with the probability of the union of two events (Event A OR Event B).General Formula: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$Note: Subtract $P(A \cap B)$ to avoid double-counting the overlap.For Mutually Exclusive Events: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$ (since the intersection is 0).Example: Tossing a coin. $P(\text{Head or Tail}) = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1$.2. Multiplication RuleApplies to the intersection of two events (Event A AND Event B).For Independent Events: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$Example: Rolling a die. $P(\text{even number AND number } < 4)$:$P(\text{even}) = 3/6 = 1/2$$P(< 4) = 3/6 = 1/2$$P(A \cap B) = 1/2 \times 1/2 = 1/4$3. Complement RuleDeals with the probability of an event NOT occurring.Formula: $P(A') = 1 - P(A)$Example: Probability of not drawing a heart from a deck of 52 cards.$P(\text{heart}) = 13/52 = 1/4$$P(\text{not heart}) = 1 - 1/4 = 3/4$4. Conditional ProbabilityThe probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred.Formula: $P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$Example: Probability card is a heart given it's a face card ($B$).$P(\text{face card}) = 12/52$$P(\text{heart and face card}) = 3/52$ (King, Queen, Jack of hearts)$P(A \mid B) = (3/52) / (12/52) = 3/12 = 1/4$Solved QuestionsQ1 (Two coins): Probability of exactly one head?Logic: $1 - P(HH) - P(TT) = 1 - 1/4 - 1/4 = 1/2$.Q2 (No replacement): 5 red, 3 blue balls. Probability of 1 red then 1 blue?Logic: $P(\text{red}) \times P(\text{blue after red}) = 5/8 \times 3/7 = 15/56$.Q3 (Die): Probability of number divisible by 2 OR 3?Logic: $P(/2) + P(/3) - P(/6) = 1/2 + 1/3 - 1/6 = 5/6$.Q4 (Cards): Probability of face card OR heart?Logic: $P(\text{face}) + P(\text{heart}) - P(\text{face and heart}) = 12/52 + 13/52 - 3/52 = 22/52 = 11/26$.Q5 (Two dice): Probability of sum $> 9$?Logic: Outcomes are (4,6), (5,5), (5,6), (6,4), (6,5), (6,6). Total = $6/36 = 1/6$.Practice AnswersDrawing red or green (5 red, 3 green, 2 blue): 0.8Two heads in a row: 0.25Not drawing a heart: 0.75Probability a face card is a spade: 0.25 Note: The Multiplication Rule for dependent events is actually the definition of Conditional Probability rearranged: $P(A \cap B) = P(B) \times P(A|B)$. Don't just multiply $P(A) \times P(B)$ if the first event changes the sample space for the second!


Conditional Probability

Conditional probability refers to the likelihood of an event occurring given a specific condition or prior knowledge of another event.
It is the likelihood of an event occurring, given that another event has already occurred. In probability, this is denoted as A given B, expressed as P(A | B), indicating the probability of event A when the event B has already occurred.
This is the heart of most machine learning models (like Naive Bayes). Here is the trick: you aren't just calculating a probability; you are shrinking the sample space to only the world where event $B$ has already happened.Here is the 1:1 extraction of the content from the "Conditional Probability" page.DefinitionConditional probability is the likelihood of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred. It is denoted as $P(A \mid B)$, read as "Probability of A given B."The FormulaThe probability of $B$ occurring when $A$ has already occurred:$$P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}$$Where:$P(A \cap B)$ is the probability of both events occurring simultaneously.$P(A)$ is the probability of the condition (the "given" event).Key PropertiesSample Space Logic: $P(S \mid A) = P(A \mid A) = 1$.Union Rule: $P((A \cup B) \mid X) = P(A \mid X) + P(B \mid X) - P((A \cap B) \mid X)$.Non-Commutative: $P(A \mid B) \neq P(B \mid A)$ (generally).Complement Rule: $P(A' \mid B) = 1 - P(A \mid B)$. Note: $P(A \mid B') \neq 1 - P(A \mid B)$.Independent vs. Dependent EventsIndependent: If $A$ and $B$ are independent, the condition tells you nothing new.$P(A \mid B) = P(A)$$P(B \mid A) = P(B)$Dependent: The occurrence of $A$ changes the probability of $B$.Comparison TableFeatureConditional ProbabilityJoint ProbabilityMarginal ProbabilityFocusProb. of A given B happenedProb. of A and B both happeningProb. of A happening aloneNotation$P(A \mid B)$$P(A \cap B)$$P(A)$ScopeTwo or more eventsTwo or more eventsSingle eventSolved ExamplesExample 1 (Cards): Prob. of drawing a red card on 2nd draw ($B$) given 1st was red ($A$) without replacement.Solution: After 1st red, 25 red cards and 51 total remain. $P(B \mid A) = 25/51 \approx 0.49$.Example 2 (Sports): 70% play football (A), 60% play basketball (B), 40% play both. Prob. a basketball player also plays football?Solution: $P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{40}{60} = 2/3$.Example 3 (Dice): Roll twice. Given sum is even, prob. 1st roll was even?Solution: Sum is even in 18 cases (9 even+even, 9 odd+odd). Cases where 1st is even and sum is even: 9. Prob = $9/18 = 1/2$.Example 4 (Rain/Umbrella): Rain ($R$) = 30%, Carry Umbrella ($U$) = 50%. When it rains, you carry umbrella 80% of the time ($P(U \mid R)$). Prob it's raining given you have an umbrella?Logic: $P(R \cap U) = P(R) \times P(U \mid R) = 0.3 \times 0.8 = 0.24$.Result: $P(R \mid U) = \frac{P(R \cap U)}{P(U)} = \frac{0.24}{0.50} = 48\%$ . Bayes Theorem in Machine learning

Bayes' Theorem is just a way to "update" your initial hunch (Prior) based on new data (Likelihood). In Machine Learning, we use this to turn features into a prediction.Here is the 1:1 extraction of the content from the "Bayes Theorem in Machine Learning" page.DefinitionBayes' Theorem explains how to update the probability of a hypothesis ($A$) when new evidence ($B$) is observed. It combines prior knowledge with data to make decisions under uncertainty.Mathematical Formulation$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) P(A)}{P(B)}$$Posterior Probability $P(A \mid B)$: The updated probability of hypothesis $A$ after observing evidence $B$.Likelihood $P(B \mid A)$: The probability of observing evidence $B$ assuming hypothesis $A$ is true.Prior Probability $P(A)$: The initial belief about hypothesis $A$ before seeing evidence.Evidence (Marginal Likelihood) $P(B)$: The total probability of observing evidence $B$, acting as a normalization factor.For Multiple Hypotheses ($n$ Events)If you have multiple mutually exclusive hypotheses $\{E_1, E_2, \dots, E_n\}$ and an observation $O$:$$P(E_i \mid O) = \frac{P(O \mid E_i) P(E_i)}{\sum_{j=1}^{n} P(O \mid E_j) P(E_j)}$$Step-by-Step Implementation (Naive Bayes)The article outlines a Python implementation for Spam detection:Libraries: Use pandas, scikit-learn (MultinomialNB, CountVectorizer).Preprocessing: Map labels ('ham': 0, 'spam': 1).Vectorization: Use CountVectorizer to convert text into word frequency vectors (numerical form).Training: Fit the MultinomialNB model on training data.Evaluation: Use a Confusion Matrix, Accuracy Score, and Classification Report (Precision, Recall, F1).Applications in Machine LearningNaive Bayes Classifier: A probabilistic model assuming feature independence. Efficient for text classification/spam detection.Bayes Optimal Classifier: A theoretical model that predicts the class with the highest posterior probability:$$\hat{y} = \arg \max_{y} P(y \mid x)$$Bayesian Optimization: Used for finding the maximum/minimum of expensive functions (e.g., hyperparameter tuning).Bayesian Belief Networks (BBNs): Graphical models representing conditional dependencies using a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG).Key Benefits in MLHandles uncertainty and noisy data effectively.Enables learning even with limited data.Supports probabilistic interpretation of model predictions.Quick Quiz SummaryMain Use: Update hypothesis probability after new evidence.Role of $P(B)$: Acts as a normalization factor.Bayes Optimal Classifier: Predicts the class with the highest posterior probability.Bayesian Optimization: Primarily for hyperparameter tuning. Note: In the "Naive" version of Bayes, we assume features are independent (e.g., the presence of the word "Money" is independent of the word "Free"). In the real world, they aren't, but the "trick" is that the classifier still performs surprisingly well even with this false assumption!

Probability Distributions in Data Science

1. Fundamental ConceptsBefore documenting specific distributions, these three functions are the "DNA" of how we describe data spread:Probability Mass Function (PMF): Used for Discrete variables. It gives the probability of an exact, countable value (e.g., $P(X=3)$).Probability Density Function (PDF): Used for Continuous variables. It shows the relative likelihood of a variable falling within a range. The probability of a single exact point is 0; probability is instead the area under the curve.Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF): Used for both. It calculates the probability that a variable is less than or equal to a specific value: $F_X(x) = P(X \leq x)$.Formula: $F_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t) \, dt$ (where $f(t)$ is the PDF).2. Discrete Probability DistributionsUsed when outcomes are countable whole numbers (e.g., number of clicks, number of defects).A. Bernoulli DistributionDefinition: The simplest distribution. Models a single trial with exactly two outcomes: Success (1) or Failure (0).PMF Formula: $P(X = x) = p^x (1-p)^{1-x}, \quad x \in \{0, 1\}$Example: A single coin flip or a single user clicking an ad.B. Binomial DistributionDefinition: Models the number of successes ($k$) in a fixed number of independent Bernoulli trials ($n$).PMF Formula: $P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$Example: Flipping a coin 10 times and counting the number of heads.C. Poisson DistributionDefinition: Models the number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time or space, given a known constant average rate ($\lambda$).PMF Formula: $P(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}$Example: Number of customers entering a coffee shop per hour.D. Geometric DistributionDefinition: Models the number of trials required to achieve the first success.PMF Formula: $P(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1} p$Example: Number of marketing emails sent before a customer finally makes a purchase.3. Continuous Probability DistributionsUsed when the variable can take any real value within a range (e.g., height, time, price).A. Normal (Gaussian) DistributionDefinition: The "Bell Curve." Symmetrical distribution where most observations cluster around the central peak (mean).PDF Formula: $f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$Parameters: $\mu$ (mean), $\sigma$ (standard deviation).Usage: Basis for many ML algorithms; used for feature scaling and anomaly detection.Shutterstock Explore B. Uniform DistributionDefinition: Every outcome in a specified range $[a, b]$ has an equal probability.PDF Formula: $f(x) = \frac{1}{b-a}, \quad a \leq x \leq b$Usage: Random sampling and initialization of weights in neural networks.C. Exponential DistributionDefinition: Models the time or distance between independent events occurring at a constant average rate.PDF Formula: $f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \ge 0$Usage: Survival analysis, predicting time until a server fails.D. Beta DistributionDefinition: Defined on the interval $[0, 1]$. It represents a distribution of probabilities.Parameters: $\alpha$ and $\beta$ (shape parameters).Usage: Bayesian inference, A/B testing, and modeling uncertainty in CTR (Click-Through Rates).E. Log-Normal DistributionDefinition: A distribution whose logarithm is normally distributed. It only models positive values and is right-skewed.Usage: Modeling stock prices, income distribution, and multiplicative growth processes.F. Chi-Square DistributionDefinition: Used primarily in hypothesis testing to determine the "goodness of fit" or independence between categorical variables.Parameter: $k$ (degrees of freedom).G. Gamma DistributionDefinition: A generalization of the Exponential distribution; it models the total wait time for $k$ independent events to occur.4.
