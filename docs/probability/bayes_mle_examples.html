Bayes' Theorem & MLE: Practical Examples

To solidify your understanding as an ML engineer, here are two classic problems: one focused on updating beliefs (Bayes) and one on finding optimal parameters (MLE).

Examples in This Page
Example 1 — Bayes' Theorem in Spam Filtering Example 2 — MLE for a Bernoulli Distribution (Coin Toss) Key Takeaway for ML Engineers
1
Bayes' Theorem in Spam Filtering
Problem

A certain email filter is tested.

  • 10% of all emails are Spam \((P(S) = 0.10)\).
  • If an email is Spam, the word "Offer" appears 80% of the time \((P(O|S) = 0.80)\).
  • If an email is Not Spam (Ham), the word "Offer" appears only 5% of the time \((P(O|H) = 0.05)\).

Question: If you receive an email containing the word "Offer," what is the probability that it is actually Spam?

We need to find \(P(S|O)\). Using Bayes' Theorem:

$$P(S|O) = \frac{P(O|S) \cdot P(S)}{P(O)}$$
Step 1: Calculate the Evidence P(O)

The word "Offer" can appear in both Spam and Ham emails:

$$P(O) = [P(O|S) \cdot P(S)] + [P(O|H) \cdot P(H)]$$ $$P(O) = (0.80 \cdot 0.10) + (0.05 \cdot 0.90) = 0.08 + 0.045 = 0.125$$
Step 2: Plug into the Formula
$$P(S|O) = \frac{0.80 \cdot 0.10}{0.125} = \frac{0.08}{0.125} = 0.64$$
Conclusion: Even though the word "Offer" is very common in spam, there is a 64% chance the email is spam. The "Prior" \((10\%)\) was low, which pulls the final probability down from the \(80\%\) likelihood.
2
MLE for a Bernoulli Distribution (Coin Toss)
Problem

You are given a coin, but you don't know if it's fair. You flip it 10 times and observe 7 Heads (H) and 3 Tails (T).

Question: Find the Maximum Likelihood Estimate for the probability of getting a Head \((\theta)\).

For a single flip, the probability is \(P(x) = \theta^x (1-\theta)^{1-x}\) (where \(x=1\) for Heads and \(x=0\) for Tails).

Step 1: Likelihood Function L(θ)

Since the flips are independent:

$$L(\theta) = \theta^7 \cdot (1-\theta)^3$$
Step 2: Log-Likelihood ℓ(θ)
$$\ell(\theta) = \ln(\theta^7 \cdot (1-\theta)^3) = 7\ln(\theta) + 3\ln(1-\theta)$$
Step 3: Find the Maximum

Differentiate with respect to \(\theta\) and set to \(0\):

$$\frac{d}{d\theta} [7\ln(\theta) + 3\ln(1-\theta)] = \frac{7}{\theta} - \frac{3}{1-\theta} = 0$$
Step 4: Solve for θ
$$\frac{7}{\theta} = \frac{3}{1-\theta}$$ $$7(1-\theta) = 3\theta \implies 7 - 7\theta = 3\theta \implies 7 = 10\theta \implies \theta = 0.7$$
Conclusion: The MLE for the parameter \(\theta\) is 0.7. This makes intuitive sense: if you see \(70\%\) heads in your sample, the "most likely" version of the world is one where the coin has a \(70\%\) chance of landing on heads.

Key Takeaway for ML Engineers

When to use Bayes

Bayes is used when you have a Prior (e.g., in Naive Bayes classifiers or Bayesian Neural Networks).

When to use MLE

MLE is used when you want the best-fit parameters for your data without assuming any prior belief (e.g., training a Logistic Regression or finding the weights in a standard Neural Network).
